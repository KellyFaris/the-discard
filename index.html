The Discard Moment

You've been holding that tile for four turns. You know you shouldn't. Your hand is going nowhere, your wall is almost gone, and the person across from you is discarding way too confidently for someone who hasn't won in three weeks.

You let it go. Someone calls it immediately. Of course they do.

Here's the thing: that moment — that specific, sinking, oh no moment — is shared by every single person who has ever sat down at a mahjong table. Beginner or veteran. It doesn't matter. The tiles humble us all. That's why we're here.

Welcome to The Discard. A newsletter for the group chat, the weeknight table, the player who just learned what a pung is and the one who's been playing for thirty years. Pull up a chair.

Tile Tip
For Every Level
🌿 This Week's Tip

Stop falling in love with your hand. It's the number one thing that separates a player who wins from a player who almost wins. You spotted a gorgeous hand on the card in the first Charleston — two flowers, a pair, everything lining up. Beautiful. And then the tiles stopped coming. The smart move is to pivot. The fun move is to keep chasing. The newsletter move is to remind you: the card doesn't care about your vision board.

For beginners: it can feel like there's only one "right" hand to play. There isn't. Part of learning is recognizing when to cut your losses and chase something that's actually alive in the wall. The sooner you make peace with changing direction, the more fun you'll have.

For experienced players: you already know this. And yet. 😂

💬 This Issue's Question

"I've been playing for about six months and I still feel like I'm the slowest one at the table. Everyone else seems to just know what to discard. Am I doing something wrong?"

You're not doing anything wrong — you're doing exactly what six months in looks like. Here's the thing nobody tells beginners: experienced players aren't faster because they think less. They're faster because they've already had the same internal argument you're having right now about a thousand times, and they know how it ends.

The speed comes from pattern recognition, not confidence. The more hands you see — at the table, in practice, just flipping through the card — the faster your brain starts matching tiles to possibilities without you having to consciously walk through every option. It's not a shortcut. It's just repetition doing its job.

Give yourself the full year. You'll surprise yourself.
